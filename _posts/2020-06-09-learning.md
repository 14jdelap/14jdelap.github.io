# How can we learn more effectively?

I never gave much thought how I learnt and how effective I was until about a year and a half ago. I was doing a job I wasn't trained to do, with colleagues who did, and I wasn't learning fast enough.

Maybe you've never been in such a situation, but everyone has the same situation: how effective you are at learning has a huge role in determining your outcome. The marginal effect of learning more effectively over the long-term is huge because learning compounds.

## Memory
### How do our memories work?

There are 2 broad types of memory: the working memory and long-term memory. The working memory is like a blackboard where we place ideas: small and temporary. There are 4 to 5 "slots" within the working memory. There aren't more because of our evolutionary need to filter and sort information. This is why it can be hard to focus in distracting environments: external stimuli take slots of working memory.

The limitations of our working memories make chunking so important. Chunking is the associating of information that is similar through meaning (e.g. apartment, house and loft) or use  (e.g. red, green and blue).

Chunking coils information together more tightly. The better formed the chunk, the more information is coiled. This coiling of information means we can retrieve information with less working memory. The larger the chunks, the more information we'll be able to handle in a given moment.

What's interesting is that chunks apply to everything. You use chunks to kick a ball, to dance salsa, to advanced math and learning a new language.

Yet we also have a need to store information for the long-run. If we only use the blackboards of our working memories, we'd be like Leonard in Memento.

This is where our long-term memory comes in. They are more like a warehouse: they store information for the long-term. We tend to underestimate the power of long-term memory because it isn't immediately obvious. Think of your life: can't you remember the last time you were surprised at remembering something you learned years ago?

### Implications for learning

How we approach information is critical to storing it. As a professional crammer at university, it took me 2 to 3 weeks to study the yearly content of a course. Yet I always had a feeling of forgetfulness after each exam. 

Every time I wrote an exam I felt I was flushing that information. It was like a devil's pact to get good grades at the expense of not remembering much. I even topped a 60+ person course in my junior year that I can't remember much of today! I'm not sure if even after the exam I would have been able to give a convincing answer, as paradoxical as it sounds.

Why this happened is obvious now: cramming is a sure-fire way to forget. In contrast, spaced learning is an excellent method to remember (Ebbinghaus, 1885).

This is the first important concept: spacing the repetition of content through time to improve retention.

One of the less intuitive nuances of memory is our senses play an important role. How well you remember depends on the role of your senses in the experiences. It's about how you used your sense of smell, touch, hearing, taste and sight in a situation. People with poorer memories generally don't have bad memories. Rather, they tend to not be recording sensory information well.

This is the backbone of an ancient memorization technique called the Memory Palace. It is about associating a piece of information —say, an equation or fact— with a location. It works by tapping into our spatial relationship memories. Since we remember well locations that we know, we link information with those places. Our ability to remember increases when we exaggerate the experience. It is also linked with our senses. Aim to smell or hear or see the trigger that associates the information and the location. This is why drawing or sounds help us remember.

### Forming memory chunks
Chunks form when we associate different pieces of information through meaning or use. In our brains, chunks are the physical synaptic connections between neurons. These synaptic connections have different strengths. It all depends on how often we've associated the pieces of information. The more we've done so, the more pronounced the connection and the chunk. As this suggests, chunks are not static and can grow with practice. The inverse is also true: chunks can weaken with lack of practice. Moreover, chunks that have deep connections are easier to retrieve.

We form chunks in 3 steps: working with focused attention, understanding, and practice. Focusing is important because that's when our brains use our working memory. In this step, we take in new information and associate it with our current knowledge. In that sense, we under appreciate the concept of transfer. This comes into play when we learn new information that relates to something we already know. Familiarity with a similar concept means new information is easier to understand and chunk. This is the heart of the argument in favor of generalists from thinkers like David Epstein.

We also underplay the importance of understanding in learning. Look at someone who does math without understanding why or in what context the tool works. This conceptual nuance also helps you chunk and remember. Yet beyond memory, it also allows you to be more strategic of how you use the information you remember.  

There are useful tactics to improve understanding that draw from transfer. Metaphors, stories, and allegories make a concept less abstract and more relatable. What is crucial is that the more esoteric the concept, the more important these tools are.

## Illusions of understanding

Yet the biggest threat is likely an illusion of understanding. This comes from mediocre studying habits. Reading passively and highlighting text rather than engaging with it. Working on problems without understanding your approach and the nuances. Deceiving yourself by reading the answers rather than testing yourself.

More subtle, but no less important, is how we engage the material. Are we testing for extremes? Finding connections? Are we synthesizing what we're learning? Reorganizing it? Picturing it? How does it fit with what I already know? These are learning habits we need to exercise.

The third and final step to learning is practice. Without repeated practice, chunks cannot form. Remember that our brains filter information. We need to deep neural connections to make sure information we want to keep stays present. Yet as suggested before, it's not only a question of practice but of how we practice.

I'd argue there are 3 broad related strokes to this. First, practicing as a tool to help us gauge our illusion of competence. This includes recalling information we learnt and doing practice questions. The second is the idea of deliberate practice and practicing what is most difficult for us. It ensures we are able to learn holistically and reduces illusions of competence. The third is spacing out practice through time to improve retention.

Chunking is necessary but insufficient to gain mastery. We need to understand, remember, and use large amounts of information. But chunking does not involve using that information with creativity or flexibility.

For mastery, what's important is to interweave the learning to have more flexibility. Interweaving involves revising the material in different orders and using different learning techniques. It also means learning in different locations because what we remember is also tied to where we are.

Interweaving uses the diffused mode of the mind. So to optimize, it's helpful to focus on improving our diffused thinking. Josh Waitzkin, for example, revisits his hardest task in the evening and sleeps on it. This is useful as while we sleep our diffused mode is at work.  A critical step is avoiding other inputs between the task and going to sleep. Once he wakes up he goes back to work on it before getting any other inputs, such as from his phone. Other ways to tap on the diffused mode are through exercise or meditation.

Yet this is the last mile. Running or meditation does not substitute working with focused attention and forming chunks. This would be like trying to work land that has no seeds — don't expect many crops to come out of it.

Carol Dwerk's research on entity and incremental mindsets is important for this. This is because what differentiates the best is that they persist. The rest tend to stop from frustration. In essence, they manage their emotions better during the learning process.

Intelligence does not play a productive role in developing growth mindsets. It may even play a negative one, as Waitzkin observed in his stellar book The Art of Learning. He saw that children leaving competitive chess tend to be the intelligent ones. These were the ones with early successes.  He hypothesizes that it could be due to an identity problem. People who identify as "smart" or "winners" also judge themselves more when they fail to win. This clash between their identities and the outcome can sap their enthusiasm. In contrast, the ones who struggle to develop their game need to overcome more obstacles.  In this sense, learning is not only about the work itself or even about how we handle our emotions. Learning is also about our identities. When we struggle on things we're "good" at, we'll discourage. And if we're "bad" at something, we won't even try.

But what makes exceptional performance? Waitzkins says:

"First, what is the difference that allows some to fit into that narrow window to the top? And second, what is the point? If ambition spells probable disappointment, why pursue excellence? In my opinion, the answer to both questions lies in a well-thought-out approach that inspires resilience, and day-to-day enjoyment of the process. The vast majority of motivated people, young and old, make terrible mistakes in their approach to learning. They fall frustrated by the wayside while those on the road to success keep steady on their paths."

### Practical implications

We've seen how memory and learning works and what are some tools and techniques to improve how we use them. As with anything, you need to focus on the basics to go anywhere.

First, make sure you get enough sleep. Our brains generate toxins through the day like we release CO2 from breathing oxygen. An important function of sleep is to wash away these toxins, helping you recover and focus the next day. A study found that people with 24 consecutive hours without sleep were as impaired as a drunkard. But it's more than that. It also hinders our ability to learn and remember information. Sleep consolidates information in our memories. Not sleeping means we don't.

Exercise is also critical. It helps us tap into our diffuse state and improve the functioning of our brains. It reduces stress and fatigue. It also improves sleep quality and energy levels. If it weren't enough, it helps keep blood, glucose and oxygen levels in the brain high. These benefits of sleep seem to relate to the hippocampus. It helps us process and retrieve declarative and spatial memories. These are the memories that relate to facts and events.  and spatial relationship memories, which involve routes and paths. Exercise is also important because it seems to increase the size of the hippocampus. This growth also suggests that neurons can grow in the hippocampus after childhood. Growing evidence supports this.

There is also a subtle psychological dimension for performance — especially world-class performance. It has to do with the intersection of our conscious and subconscious selves. This is about being present  and at ease with your emotions. Roberto Baggio didn't miss the penalty in the 1994 World Cup because of lack of practice. He missed it because there was an emotional friction. That hampered his system 1, as Kahneman calls the part of the brain that acts automatically). This also ties with our working memories because some emotions can affect our focus. Or has anger or fear never distracted you?

What's tied with this is the importance of habits and overcoming procrastination. Some tools are well-known and proven.  One of these is the Pomodoro technique, which rests on 2 ideas. First, that  we need to overcome a small initial discomfort to start being productive. Second, that small breaks to recover will contribute to performance. Another tactic is to control your environment, which James Clear has popularized. The idea is since our environment affects our working memory we need to optimize it. There are many moving parts to it: noise, light, habit triggers. If it seems insignificant, take in mind this: a 10 decibel increase in noise leads to 5% less productivity!

Another popular technique is priming as a tool for peak performance. It's about a self-reinforcing routine that ties to our subconscious. There's lots to unpack in this idea of peak performance, so look into it if you're interested — I can't cover it here. Partial substitutes are meditation and heart rate variation training. Both calm you down and make you more conscientious of the moment. By extension, they help you enter periods of deep work and help you tap your unconscious.

These lessons are rewarding in themselves, but won't take you as far as they could without focus. There are several dimensions to this. You need focused attention to benefit of diffused thinking, sleep and exercise. Yet there's a more important dimension. What you focus your time on and how systematic you are in doing so affects shapes your career and life. No optimization will change your outcome if you aren't going in the right direction. This means sticking to long-term objectives and making the right trade-offs. In the spirit of Michael Porter, the essence of strategy is choosing what not to do.

What should we focus on? How should we spend our time? Some things can be learned from others, but some answers lie within us.
